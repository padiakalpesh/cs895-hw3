Investigating if student-teacher ratio influences SAT scores

This assignment required us to develop a Tableau dashboard to visualize student-teacher ratios of various US states and corresponding SAT scores in order to investigate if SAT scores are influenced by smaller ratios.

Insights

Each animated representation above visualizes the effect of student-teacher ratio on SAT scores. While the first visualization represents SAT score as a bubble, the second visualization plots the ratio against SAT score for each state, with the states sorted in ascending order of mean SAT score. The second visualization also plots the mean SAT score for United States.

We observe large number of green bubbles in the first visualization representing multiple states have low student-teacher ratio. We are interested in looking at larger bubbles as these represent high SAT scores. It is interesting that for all three years (2000, 2005 and 2006) the number of large green bubbles is almost twice that of red green bubbles, suggesting that a low student to teacher ratio may result in higher SAT scores. Also noticable is the drop in SAT scores from 2000 to 2005 in the states of Utah, Nevada and Oregon as the ratio increases while an increase in SAT scores in the states of North Dakota, South Dakota, Missouri and Alabama as the ratio decreases.

An even more interesting observation is made for data available from 2006 for which the percentage of SAT takers is also available. This information is visible on the map as a gray gradient with dark regions indicating higher percentage of SAT takers. We observe that while the state of Maine has 100% representation and lower ratio, the average SAT scores are less. On the other hand, states of North Dakota have poorer represenataion and consequently higher scores for ratios in the same range. Again however, looking at the western states, we notice a fairly moderate percent of test takers and moderate values of mean SAT scores as well as ratios. Thus, it feels that percent of SAT takers is also a factor affecting the mean SAT scores.

In the second visualization, we are interested in looking at number of greener points towards the right of United States mean score. We indeed see that for all years, this number is sufficiently large. We also see interesting cases like Ohio state where small percent of SAT takers in 2006 (27%) with increased student-teacher ratio results in drop in mean scores. Similar observation is made for Utah, Idaho and Arizona.

Looking at above patterns, we can say that lower ratios contribute towards better SAT scores in general, though percentage of students taking SAT also effects mean SAT score (a higher percentage of test takers lowers the mean).

 

Method

Since the data was available only for select years in each data set, rather than taking a mean of all years, I decided to take only those years for which both data was available to visualize the relation. This also enabled me to compare the growth over time. (Though I understand that inferring such a growth from just 3 data points - years 2000, 2005 and 2006 - is not completely flawless, nevertheless it allows us to predict a trend - whether data points are moving up or down).

Also, since for the year 2000 SAT had only two sections, while for the other years there were three sections, I decided to take a mean of combined score by adding score in all sections for that year and dividing by number of sections. This normalized all scores to the range of [0-800] and allowed for easier comparison.

For the year 2006 I also wanted to plot the percent of SAT takers as a dimension on the map. Since Tableau by default doesn't allow creating custom map layers, I had to either design my own and serve from a WMS server or somehow embed the information in graph itself.

I decided to go with the first approach initially but after spending few hours in setting up a WMS server and writing my own data layers, I quit. Actually, this option would have worked wonderful if I had to embed generic geographical information, but since I wanted to encode only specific data for a specific year, this approach was too taxing.

I then decided to embed the data regarding percentage of SAT takers in my initial data set and decided to render it as a data point. This method required me to do two things. Firstly, I had to obtain geographical boundry information for states of the USA, something I found on the US census bureau site here. (I was unable to obtain/parse data for Alaska and Hawaii successfully).

Next, I had to use this information to create records in my dataset each represnting a line on the map to create a filled polygon. I also had to manually perform an outer join of my data and this polygonal information as using Tableau to join the two data resulted in lot of null values - there is only inner, left and right join in Tableau.

I had to do this for each year I had to chart as I wanted to display the polygons only for year 2006 when the percentage of SAT takers is available. The resultant record set exceeded 65535 rows, forcing me to dump all data into CSV files. These files can be downloaded here: sat_ratio_combined4_sheet1.csv and sat_ratio_combined4_sheet3_1.csv .

After loading these files into Tableau, I created a Double Dual Axis graph to view latitude and longitudes in the shape of USA's boundary, synchronized the two axes and removed headers. I also enabled multiple markers and created polygonal layers for one set of lat/long and bubbles for another. This gave me the representation that is visible now minus the background map. So, I took a screenshot of the map with no data points (only lat/long), and used that as a background image for my DDA graph. This resulted in the final world map with polygonal layers for 2006.

To create the second visualization, states representing X-axis were sorted in ascending order of their mean SAT scores. This allowed me to compare the performance against national mean (represented as United States). A normal X-Y graph was used to represnt this information with additional dimensions used to create marks.
